# Special operations and regulatory limitations

Some E1A questions ask who may operate, who is accountable, or whose permission is needed. Separate these roles carefully. A US-registered vessel in international waters may carry an amateur station operated under an FCC-issued amateur license. When the station is aboard a US-documented or registered vessel or craft, physical control must be held by a person with an FCC amateur operator license or by someone authorized for alien reciprocal operation. Operation aboard a ship or aircraft also requires approval from the ship's master or the aircraft's pilot in command. [47 CFR §§ 97.5(a)(2) and 97.11(a)]

In a message-forwarding system, an automatically forwarded rule violation remains primarily attributable to the control operator of the station where the improper communication originated. Forwarding equipment does not move primary accountability to every later station in the path. [47 CFR § 97.219(d)]

Power questions on the low-frequency bands use EIRP, not transmitter PEP. EIRP accounts for the complete radiating system relative to an isotropic radiator. The 2200-meter band limit is 1 watt EIRP. The 630-meter limit is generally 5 watts EIRP, with a lower limit in specified parts of Alaska. Read the unit as part of the rule: an answer expressed as PEP is not equivalent merely because its number appears familiar. [47 CFR §§ 97.313(k) and 97.313(l)]

For rule-based exam questions, first classify the issue—operator authority, permission, accountability, or power—then locate the matching rule and its defined unit.
