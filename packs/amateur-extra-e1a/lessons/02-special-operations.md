# Special operations and regulatory limitations

Some E1A questions ask who may operate, who is accountable, or whose permission is needed. Separate these roles carefully. A US-registered vessel in international waters may carry an amateur station under FCC amateur authority. When the station is aboard a US-documented or registered vessel or craft, physical control must be held by a person named in an amateur station license grant or by someone authorized for alien reciprocal operation. Operation aboard a ship or aircraft also requires approval from the ship's master or the aircraft's pilot in command. [47 CFR §§ 97.5(a)(2) and 97.11(a)]

In a message-forwarding system, an automatically forwarded rule violation remains primarily attributable to the control operator of the station where the improper communication originated. A forwarding station that retransmits a violative communication inadvertently is generally not accountable for that communication, subject to the first-forwarding-station requirements in the rule. [47 CFR §§ 97.219(b)–(d)]

Power questions on the low-frequency bands use EIRP, not transmitter PEP. EIRP accounts for the complete radiating system relative to an isotropic radiator. The 2200-meter band limit is 1 watt EIRP. The 630-meter limit is generally 5 watts EIRP, with a lower limit in specified parts of Alaska. Read the unit as part of the rule: an answer expressed as PEP is not equivalent merely because its number appears familiar. [47 CFR §§ 97.313(k) and 97.313(l)]

For rule-based exam questions, first classify the issue—operator authority, permission, accountability, or power—then locate the matching rule and its defined unit.
